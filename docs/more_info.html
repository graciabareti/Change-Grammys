Impact of Black Artist

HipHop/Rap and R&B artists make up most of the black nominees for the Grammys and research has found trends of bias within the Grammys towards black artists especially within such genres. The University of Southern California’s Annenberg Inclusion initiative found that Black performers represented about 38% of all artists on Billboard’s signature chart from 2012 to 2020, yet they received only 26.7% of top Grammy nominations during the same period1. Such statistics demonstrate how Black artists are not being awarded to the caliber that they should, causing many artists such as The Weeknd and Drake to boycott the Grammys2.

3

When looking at the Grammys Big Four categories of Album of The Year, Record of The Year, Song of The Year, and Best New Artist, over the last nine years there have only been four black artists to win within these categories4. In that time we’ve had classic albums such as Lemonade, DAMN, and songs such as Blinded Lights being subjugated to mere nominations.

The visualization breaks down Grammy Award-winning songs that are also Billboard charting from 1999-2019 based on genre. The visualization demonstrates that rap and r&b songs perform just as well, if better than any other genre of music when considering other music ranking-based outlets. Despite the results of the visualization, if we were to look solely at Grammy Awards, we can anticipate an increase in awards within every other genre but hip hop/rap and r&b.

Record Label’s Role

#Groups RiaaStatus accolades(ex.gold, platinum,etc.) into specific range categories,
#that are either greater than or less than 5 for a given status
Label_New <- single_status %>% 
  separate(RiaaStatus, into = c("Number", "Level"), sep = "x") %>% 
  mutate(Level = ifelse(is.na(Level), Number, Level)) %>% 
  mutate(Number = parse_number(Number),
         Number = ifelse(Number >= 5, ">5", "<5")) %>% 
  mutate(status= ifelse(!is.na(Number), paste0(Number, Level), Level))

Label_Grammy<-Label_New %>%
right_join(song_results, by ="Name")%>%
drop_na(status)
#Counts the amount of accolades within a specific status range for each given label 
Status_New<-Label_Grammy%>%
group_by(Label, status)%>%
summarize(count=n())

# Filters out the status and count for the five record labels we aimed to focus on
final_status<-Status_New%>%
filter(Label %in% c("Republic Records", "Fueled by Ramen", "Def Jam", 
"Atlantic Records", "Columbia"))

#Creates circle visualization distributing the amount of accolades a given label 
#has recieved
ggplot(final_status, mapping = aes(x= "", y= count, fill = status)) +
  geom_bar(width = 1, stat  = "identity") +
  coord_polar("y",start = 0)+
  facet_wrap(~Label)+
  theme(axis.title.x=element_blank())+
  ggtitle("Notorious Record Label Stats", subtitle="Based on Grammy Winning Songs")+
theme(plot.title = element_text(face = "bold"))

For this visualization, we wanted to see how a Grammy award-winning song was related to its artist’s record label and status. It should be noted that many Grammy award winners were independent artists as well. It should also be noted that our pie charts show angles for the total number (20 songs) of status-winning songs that Atlantic Records won. We decided to keep our charts this way because we are then able to compare the quantities of awards won across all record labels at once rather than misleading with ratios alone. We can then observe that Columbia, Atlantic, and Def Jam Records have the most Grammy-winning songs of these 5 labels. Moreover, most of these songs fall under >5 multi-platinum and <5 multi-platinum for each label, except Columbia which has a majority of Gold and <5 multi-platinum songs.

Conclusion

Despite the foul play behind the Grammy Awards, don’t get it twisted. Black artists do not need the validation nor aim to receive it from their white counterparts and audience within the music industry. Yet it still doesn’t warrant the years of not getting recognized for the effort to improve their craft to further inspire other artists within the industry to be better. Black artists deserve better.
